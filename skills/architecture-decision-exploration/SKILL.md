# Architecture Decision Exploration

Turn an ambiguous requirement into a defensible architecture decision without prematurely converting it into an implementation plan.

The process is evidence-first, question-driven, status-disciplined, and adversarial in a constructive sense. Agreement is not the objective; a coherent decision that survives technical and business scrutiny is.

## Core Principles

1. **Frame before designing.** Establish the business promise, actors, desired outcomes, non-goals, constraints, and decision horizon.
2. **Trace before proposing.** Verify representative business journeys and authoritative state owners before choosing topology or dependencies.
3. **Separate epistemic states.** Never present an observation, assumption, or recommendation as an agreed decision.
4. **Ask discriminating questions.** Each question should eliminate an alternative, establish an invariant, or expose a business tradeoff.
5. **Reason across boundaries.** Include data, identity, events, caches, queues, providers, operations, support, cost, and organizational ownership.
6. **Prefer invariants over mechanisms.** Record what must remain true before prescribing how it is implemented.
7. **Challenge the preferred answer.** Seek contradictions, escape paths, failure modes, and hidden ownership before finalizing.
8. **Keep decisions digestible.** Decompose the problem into independently answerable decisions with visible dependencies.
9. **Document uncertainty honestly.** A useful design document includes open questions, exclusions, and confidence, not only conclusions.
10. **Do not drift into delivery planning.** Produce an implementation plan only when explicitly requested after the architecture baseline is accepted.

## Decision Status Vocabulary

Use these labels throughout discovery and documentation:

| Status | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `Observed` | Verified from code, runtime evidence, documentation, policy, or an authoritative stakeholder. Cite the source. |
| `Assumed` | Believed to be true but not yet verified. State the cheapest verification. |
| `Proposed` | A candidate direction with rationale and tradeoffs; not yet agreed. |
| `Decided` | Explicitly agreed by the appropriate decision owner. Record who and why. |
| `Open` | Requires evidence, ownership, or discussion before it can be decided. |
| `Excluded` | Deliberately out of scope for the current decision or release. |
| `Superseded` | Previously proposed or decided, then replaced. Preserve the reason. |

Do not infer `Decided` from silence, selection of a neighboring option, or wording generated by an agent.

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Frame the Decision

Create a discovery workbook from [the workbook template](./assets/discovery-workbook.md).

Establish:

- Business problem and measurable outcome
- Consumers and affected stakeholders
- Required customer or operator journeys
- Non-goals and release boundaries
- Regulatory, contractual, cost, timing, and organizational constraints
- Decision owner and required approvers
- Decision deadline and expected artifact

If the request is framed as a solution, restate the underlying problem before evaluating that solution.

**Gate:** Do not compare architectures until the business promise and at least one representative journey are clear.

### Phase 2: Establish Vocabulary and Boundaries

Define ambiguous domain terms. Draw the current system boundary and identify:

- Entry points and trust boundaries
- Systems and teams inside the decision
- External providers and systems outside the organization's control
- Sources of truth and authoritative state owners
- Synchronous calls, events, jobs, callbacks, and webhooks
- Data classifications and irreversible side effects

Record disagreements in terminology as `Open`; do not work around them with inconsistent wording.

### Phase 3: Gather Evidence and Trace Journeys

Start from the most concrete anchors available: API routes, controllers, use cases, state machines, schemas, adapters, events, failing behavior, runbooks, or contractual documentation.

Trace one happy path and the most decision-relevant failure paths end to end. For every hop, record:

- Caller and callee
- Authentication and delegated identity
- Request or event contract
- Authoritative state mutation
- Idempotency and retry behavior
- External side effects
- Observability and support ownership
- Environment and tenant context

Use [the evidence guide](./references/evidence-guide.md) to distinguish verified behavior from inference.

**Gate:** Do not declare a service required, optional, or authoritative until the representative journey supports that claim or it is explicitly marked `Assumed`.

### Phase 4: Decompose the Decision

Turn the broad requirement into a decision map. Typical dimensions include:

- Product promise and required fidelity
- Actors, identity, authorization, and tenancy
- Data ownership, isolation, lifecycle, and retention
- API, event, webhook, and compatibility contracts
- Stateful workflows, idempotency, compensation, and reset
- External-provider boundaries and simulation strategy
- Deployment and environment topology
- Provisioning and readiness
- Observability, audit, support, and incident response
- Capacity, quotas, cost, and noisy-neighbor controls
- Security, privacy, compliance, and egress
- Organizational ownership and operating model

Show dependencies between decisions. Ask prerequisite questions before mechanism questions.

### Phase 5: Explore Alternatives

Develop two to four credible alternatives, including the status quo when relevant. Do not create a token alternative that no reasonable team would choose.

Evaluate each option against a stable scorecard:

- Business outcome and user experience
- Correctness and domain fidelity
- Security and isolation
- Reliability and failure containment
- Operability and supportability
- Reusability and future-platform fit
- Delivery and migration complexity
- Ongoing infrastructure and organizational cost
- Reversibility and lock-in
- Evidence confidence

Separate **architecture invariants** from **reference mechanisms**:

> Invariant: stale work cannot mutate newly reset state.  
> Reference mechanism: generation numbers on commands and events.  
> Detailed design: storage, timeout, and recovery protocol.

### Phase 6: Conduct Focused Q&A

Use [the question playbook](./references/question-playbook.md).

Ask one decision cluster at a time. For each answer:

1. Restate the answer in precise technical and business language.
2. Identify what it decides and what remains unresolved.
3. Challenge it when evidence or tradeoffs disagree.
4. Record the status immediately in the decision register.
5. Update downstream questions made obsolete by the answer.

Avoid surveying the stakeholder with a long list of implementation details. Questions should remain answerable without requiring hidden code knowledge; explain terminology before asking.

### Phase 7: Run Parallel Reviews

When the decision spans multiple boundaries, run independent read-only reviews in parallel. Useful tracks include:

- Customer/platform contract
- Shared-service and data ownership
- Security, privacy, and trust
- Reliability and operations
- Business, support, and cost

Give each reviewer the same decision baseline and require them to return:

- Supporting evidence
- Contradictions and overclaims
- Production or trust-boundary escape paths
- Missing ownership
- Questions for the other tracks

Parallel reviews inform the discussion; they do not convert proposals into decisions.

### Phase 8: Reconcile and Challenge

Before publishing, perform a contradiction review using [the review checklist](./references/review-checklist.md).

At minimum, verify:

- Every `Decided` item has an identifiable owner and explicit agreement.
- Current behavior and target behavior are not conflated.
- Diagrams agree with prose and sequence direction.
- Security claims are enforceable rather than aspirational.
- Distributed workflows do not promise impossible atomicity.
- “Parity,” “isolation,” “real-time,” and “exactly once” are qualified.
- Reset and deletion state what is cleared and what is preserved.
- External providers and customer-controlled endpoints are not treated as organizational resources.
- Open details are not hidden in confident prose.

### Phase 9: Publish Decision Artifacts

Use Markdown as the maintained source of truth. Choose the smallest artifact set that serves the audience:

- [Architecture decision record](./assets/adr-template.md) for engineering governance
- [Stakeholder design report](./assets/stakeholder-report-template.md) for broader review
- Decision register and discovery workbook for ongoing facilitation
- Diagrams for topology, trust boundaries, data flow, sequences, provisioning, and failure/reset workflows

Every final document must include:

- Executive summary
- Scope and non-goals
- Evidence and current-state observations
- Alternatives and tradeoffs
- Status-labeled decision register
- Fundamental questions and answers
- Architecture invariants
- Risks and mitigations
- Open decisions and exclusions
- Decision owners and review date

Do not produce a file-by-file implementation backlog unless the user explicitly starts a separate planning phase.

## Diagram Guidance

Use diagrams to answer questions, not decorate documents:

- **Context diagram:** actors, systems, trust and environment boundaries
- **Sequence diagram:** verified representative journey and identity propagation
- **Data ownership diagram:** sources of truth and mutation boundaries
- **Provisioning/lifecycle diagram:** readiness, reset, deletion, or migration
- **Failure diagram:** retries, idempotency, compensation, stale work, partial failure

Mark unverified edges as assumptions. Use different styling for current and proposed states. Never draw a direct call merely because the business description implies one.

## Completion Criteria

The exploration is complete enough to publish when:

- The business promise and supported journeys are explicit.
- Critical current-state flows are evidence-backed.
- Viable alternatives were compared against consistent criteria.
- Fundamental questions have status-labeled answers.
- Architecture invariants and ownership boundaries are clear.
- Independent review has challenged contradictions and overclaims.
- Open decisions and exclusions are visible and do not invalidate the selected direction.
- The document distinguishes agreed architecture from later detailed design and implementation planning.

If these conditions are not met, publish an interim discovery report rather than presenting a final architecture decision.
